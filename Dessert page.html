Malaysian Dessert

1. Ais Kacang

Ais kacang is a popular dessert in Malaysia. The most basic ingredients that was needed to make ais kacang is red beans and shaved ice. Nowadays, ais kacang often comes in vivid colours and with various fruit cocktails. Variants in Malaysia today typically include a generous portion of palm seed, red beans, sweet corn, roasted peanuts, and agar agar cubes.


2. Cendol

Cendol is an iced sweet dessert made with coconut milk, palm sugar syrup, and droplets of green rice flour jelly flavored with pandan. You could also add durian, sweetened red azuki beans, or sliced jackfruit next to the green jelly as extra toppings. Shaved ice, palm sugar, coocnut milk, and jelly noodles are the main ingredients of this dish. The caramelized flavor of cendol is derived from the combination of palm sugar and the sweetness added by the coconut milk.


3. Bubur Cha Cha

Bubur cha cha is a coconut milk dessert which is often made by using sweet potatoes, taro, sago, yams, bananas, coconut milk, pandan leaves, sugar, and salt. Some people also add water, coconut cream, and grated coconut as extra ingredients. It can be eaten hot or cold, and the ingredients are cooked in coconut milk. In Malaysia, bubur cha cha is often offered for sale as street food.


4. Tangyuan

Tangyuan is a classic Chinese dessert consisting of balls of glutinous rice served in a hot syrup or broth. They can range in size from a marble to a ping pong ball, and occasionally they have filling inside of them. Traditionally, tangyuan is consumed at the Lantern Festival as a sign of unity and reunion.


5. Apam Balik

Apam balik is a dessert originated from Fujian cuisine, that may today be found in numerous forms at roadside stalls and restaurants all around Malaysia. A blend of flour, eggs, sugar, baking soda, coconut milk, and water is used to make the pancake batter. To prevent the batter from sticking to the pan, it is fried in thick circular iron frying pan with plenty of palm margarine.


6. Kuih Loyang

Because the mold that is dipped in batter and deep-fried is made of brass (Loyang), it is called "Kuih Loyang". Kuin Loyang looks like a honeycomb. Dip the mold into the special batter and fry it in a pan. Thin, crispy, and egg-flavored, it is a traditional pastry that can only be seen during the Chinese New Year, Malay Hari Raya, or Indian Deepavali.


7. Kuih Tau Sa Piah

Kuih Tau Sa Piah, called Danwen cake in the Fujian dialect, is a crispy cake filled with mung beans, sugar, a little salt, and fried onions for seasoning, and then fried in lard or vegetable oil. The aroma of freshly baked bean paste cakes, crust, and white sesame seeds also rushed into the nose as the carton was opened. Take a gentle bite, and the aroma of roasted milk and sesame fills your mouth. The inside is sweet but not greasy. After eating, the smell of burnt cracker skin will remain in your mouth, making you unable to resist eating one piece after another.


8. Goreng Pisang

It is a method of wrapping bananas in a layer of rice flour, flour, and baking powder as a base, then adding different batter recipes according to different tastes in different places, and then deep-frying them in hot oil of up to 120 degrees until crispy. This is Goreng Pisang, one of Malaysia's special desserts. Goreng Pisang is crispy on the outside and soft on the inside. Served with specially prepared dipping sauces according to different tastes. It has become a dessert enjoyed by locals throughout Southeast Asia and South India during tea time or after meals.


9. Kuih Bahulu

Kuih Bahulu is a kind of pastry eaten by people in the Malay region, Kalimantan in Indonesia, Pontianak in West Kalimantan, Samfa County, and Singkawang. It is shaped like a madeleine cake. It is a snack made with eggs as the main raw material. It has a golden color, sweet and soft texture, is delicious and suitable for all ages, and is deeply loved and sought after by the people.


10. Roti Tisu

Roti tissue, roti tisu, or tisu prata is a sweet flatbread often sold at Mamak stalls in Malaysia and Singapore. Roti tisu is a thinner and crispier version of the traditional roti canai/prata. It is also known as "roti helikopter" (helicopter bread) because it needs to be whipped continuously and quickly during production, just like the rotating propeller of a helicopter, hence the name, and 40-50 cm round-shaped. Roti tissue is usually coated with sweet substances such as sugar and kaya (jam) or eaten with condiments such as condensed milk to increase its taste.
